# ProbeScale: Probing Analysis to Optimize Neural Scaling Laws for Efficient Small Language Model Inference 

Sourav Das 

Department of Computer Science and Engineering Indian Institution of Information Technology Kalyani Kalyani, West Bengal, India 

sourav_phd21@iiitkalyani.ac.in 

Abstract 

Small Language Models (SLMs) offer a bal-ance between capability and computational fea-sibility. Neural scaling laws inform their opti-mal training, suggesting that they possess rich internal representations that scale with their size. However, deploying even these SLMs can be challenging under strict resource constraints. Language model probing provides methods for analyzing the linguistic knowledge encoded in a model’s internals. We propose ProbeScale, a framework that unifies insights from scal-ing laws and probing to identify parameter-efficient subnetworks within pre-trained SLMs. ProbeScale utilizes the high-quality represen-tations of well-scaled SLMs and uses task-specific probes to mathematically quantify the relevance of each layer for target downstream capabilities. This allows selecting subnetworks that optimally trade off performance against parameter size. We formulate the subnetwork selection as finding a layer subset maximizing aggregated, task-weighted probe performance under a parameter budget. Experiments on rep-resentative SLMs such as RoBERTa-Large and T5-Base demonstrate that ProbeScale identifies subnetworks achieving significant parameter reduction, from 5 to 10 times, while maintain-ing high performance (95-98% of the original SLMs) on targeted tasks, outperforming heuris-tic baselines. 

1 Introduction 

The advent of Large Language Models (LLMs) has been driven by Neural Scaling Laws (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022), which empir-ically demonstrate predictable performance gains with scale (parameters N , data D, compute C). While state-of-the-art models exceed hundreds of billions to even trillions of parameters, some pre-vious works have focused on SLMs (Schick and Schütze, 2021; Lester et al., 2021) in the range of a few hundred million parameters. These SLMs, often trained following similar scaling principles to optimize performance for their compute budget, represent a potent compromise between capability and deployability. Despite their reduced size com-pared to giants like GPT-X (Brown et al., 2020), deploying even these SLMs can be prohibitive in highly resource-constrained environments, such as mobile devices or latency-critical server applica-tions. This necessitates methods for further op-timizing their efficiency for specific target tasks, moving beyond the general pre-training objective. Existing compression techniques (Han et al., 2015; Sanh et al., 2019) often operate agnosti-cally to the internal linguistic knowledge distribu-tion or rely on heuristics. Concurrently, Language Model Probing (Belinkov, 2022; Hewitt and Man-ning, 2019) provides tools to analyze where and how linguistic information (syntax, semantics, etc.) is encoded within a model’s representations, typi-cally revealing layer specialization (Jawahar et al., 2019). We propose ProbeScale , a robust framework that synergistically combines insights from Scal-ing Laws and Probing for efficient SLM infer-ence. This allows selecting subnetworks that op-timally trade off performance against parameter count |ΘS |. 2 Background and Related Work 

Scaling Laws and SLMs. Scaling laws (Hägele et al., 2024; Clark et al., 2022) establish that model performance P scales as a power law with model size N , data D, and compute C: P ≈

G(N αDβ Cγ ). These laws guide the training of optimally performing models for a given budget, including primarily SLMs in the millions parame-ter range. We utilize this by assuming our starting language model possesses high-quality, efficiently learned representations for its size N = |Θ|.

Language Model Probing. Probing investigates the information encoded in intermediate representa-tions hl by training simple classifiers gp,l (hl; ϕp,l )

to predict linguistic properties p (Alain and Ben-gio, 2016; Youssef et al., 2023). Probes have re-vealed hierarchical linguistic structure (He et al., 2024; Chen and Gao, 2022). We repurpose probe performance Vp,l from an analytical metric to a prescriptive score for component selection. 

Model Compression. Techniques like pruning (Frankle and Carbin, 2018), quantization (Xiao et al., 2023), and knowledge distillation (West et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2024) reduce model size. Struc-tured pruning (Ma et al., 2023; Ling et al., 2024) removes components like layers or heads. Layer re-moval studies (Zhang et al., 2024) often use heuris-tics (e.g., keeping top/bottom layers) or fine-tuning loss. ProbeScale differentiates itself by using tar-geted, quantitative analysis of layer function (via probing) to guide structured pruning (layer selec-tion) within well-scaled SLMs.

5 Results and Analysis 

For both SST-2 and QNLI, ProbeScale subnetworks significantly outperform the Top-k and Uniform-k baselines at equivalent parameter counts (budgets 

B). For instance, with k = 6 layers (approx. 23% of original parameters), ProbeScale retains ≈98.3% (94.9/96.5) of the original RoBERTa-Large accu-racy on SST-2 and ≈98.1% (92.9/94.7) on QNLI. This is considerably better than selecting the top 6 layers (93.8 Acc / 91.8 Acc) or uniformly spaced layers (92.5 Acc / 90.5 Acc). Even with aggressive compression to k = 4 layers ( ≈15% parameters), ProbeScale maintains high performance (93.6 Acc / 91.0 Acc), preserving ≈97.0% and ≈96.1% of the original SLM’s accuracy, respectively, again surpassing baselines. The layer relevance scores Rl,T (visualized hy-pothetically in Fig. 1) confirmed task-specific layer importance. For SST-2, relevance peaked in upper-mid layers (e.g., 16-22), while for QNLI (involv-ing syntax via POS probing), relevance was higher in mid-layers (e.g., 8-16) as well as upper layers. ProbeScale selected different contiguous blocks based on these distinct relevance profiles, explain-ing its superior task-specific adaptation compared to fixed heuristics like Top-k. Results on T5-Base (detailed in Appendix A.4) showed similar trends, confirming the approach generalizes across different SLM architectures. The performance-efficiency trade-off achieved by ProbeScale (Fig. 2) consistently lies above the curves for baseline methods, indicating a better balance. 6 Ethical Considerations 

ProbeScale operates on pre-trained SLMs and in-herits their potential biases (Gallegos et al., 2024; Bender et al., 2021). While the goal is efficiency, the extracted subnetworks MS may perpetuate these biases. Bias evaluation and mitigation remain essential for responsible deployment. The com-putational cost of the ProbeScale analysis (prob-ing) is incurred once per model/task family and is likely less than full model training or extensive distillation. The resulting efficient models reduce inference costs, potentially improving accessibility and lowering environmental impact. We used pub-lic datasets; data provenance and privacy must be considered in real-world applications. 

7 Conclusion and Future Work 

We introduced ProbeScale, a framework that uni-fies insights from Neural Scaling Laws and Lan-guage Model Probing to extract parameter-efficient subnetworks from pre-trained SLMs. By exploit-ing the quality of well-scaled SLMs and using task-specific probes to quantify layer relevance, ProbeScale selects optimal layer subsets that bal-ance performance and computational constraint. Our experiments demonstrate that ProbeScale sig-nificantly outperforms heuristic layer selection baselines, enabling substantial parameter reduction (up to 10 times) while retaining high task-specific performance (95-98%) on SLMs like RoBERTa-Large and T5-Base. Considering this is a baby 4step toward the greater goal of optimizing the lan-guage models for constrained computational setups, future work would include exploring more sophisti-cated probing techniques (e.g., analyzing attention heads, FFN layers), refining the layer relevance aggregation, investigating non-contiguous layer se-lection strategies, and extending ProbeScale to gen-erative SLMs and multi-task settings. Combining ProbeScale with orthogonal compression methods like quantization could also yield further gains.

A.5 Discussion on Probe Selection and Weighting 

The choice of probing tasks PT and weights 

w(p, T ) in Eq. 1 influences the outcome. Ideally, 

PT should cover the core linguistic phenomena required by the downstream task T . Using the downstream task itself as a probe (as done for SST-2) provides a direct measure but might overfit the selection to the specific dataset. Combining direct task probes with more general linguistic probes (like POS for QNLI) offers a balance. Determining optimal weights w could be explored via meta-learning or based on linguistic analysis of task re-quirements. In this work, we used simple heuristics (direct probe or uniform weights) which proved ef-fective.
